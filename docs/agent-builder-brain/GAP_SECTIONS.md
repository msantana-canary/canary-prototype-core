## 1. "Agent Knowledge" — the product definition and its four workstreams

**What it is.** "Agent Knowledge" (abbreviated **AK**) is the renamed, narrowed scope that came out of the **Q2 Block 2 review on May 18, 2026**. It is *not* the greenfield agent-builder the prototype explored. SJ was explicit that "Agent Studio means something different — the studio is the actual builder, in your head. I don't think this is agent builder… this is just agent knowledge." The team had previously called the effort "Brownfield agent builder," and "Agent Knowledge" stuck because it captures the real near-term job: **making the AI agents Canary already ships (voice, messaging, web chat) configurable, observable, testable, and extensible** — *brownfield*, working on top of existing agents — rather than building a brand-new "Cloud-for-Hotels agent studio" from scratch. That full greenfield builder, in the team's own words, "is currently just in our heads." The prototype in this repo is the exploration of that in-our-heads builder; AK is the shippable subset.

**The four workstreams** (confirmed in the May-18 review; they map almost one-to-one onto what this prototype already built, which is *why* the prototype was valuable even though its UI was set aside):

1. **Configuration** — "being able to configure all the AIs in the product channel: voice, messaging, web chat." This is the agent **profile / capabilities / connectors / workflow** surface of the prototype — tone, operating rules, which products an agent can use, which systems it connects to, and the step/condition logic it follows. In the prototype this is the wizard + tabbed edit view; in AK it becomes settings layered onto the *existing* voice/messaging/webchat agents.

2. **Observability** — "why does the AI say its answer? We want to be able to see the knowledge-base sources it used." This is the prototype's **Activity Feed / timeline** made truthful and inspectable: the three-layer interleaving (workflow step → capability tool-call → conversation) is exactly the "show me what the agent did and why" surface AK needs. SJ extended his copy-Claude directive specifically to this workstream — "even observability, please just copy what they did" (i.e., Claude's sources-and-reasoning view).

3. **Testing** — "if you want to tweak the AI, change the configuration, you'll be able to see that in the dashboard before you deploy." This is the prototype's **Test Sandbox (Phase A, simulated)** — persona-driven runs that animate the workflow with the active step glowing — plus the deferred Phase B (live-Claude-grounded) and Phase C (save/replay). Testing-before-deploy is the confidence gate the four-pillar research (Mar 9) flagged as central ("what if the test panel IS the product?").

4. **Custom Actions** — "the rule builder: as we move to the agent builder, what are the primitives for those rules we let you set up?" The team called this "the most agent-studio-y one" of the four — it's where a hotel adds a *new* capability/action to an existing agent and tunes it via prompt injection. This maps to the prototype's **gambit-rule / step-condition** modeling and the per-capability config modals. It's the seam between "configure what exists" (the safe AK scope) and "build something new" (the deferred greenfield).

**Capacity reality (why there's no eng to consume designs right now).** AK is a three-person team, and **~2/3 of that capacity was redirected to VoIP** for Q2 Block 2 — messaging "donated one engineer to voice for the entire block." The team was explicit that the deprioritized items "are not unimportant, just not the most important," and that the donation can't be sustained indefinitely. This capacity gap — not any failure of the design — is the proximate reason the prototype was **parked**: there is no engineering counterpart to hand Agent-Builder designs to this block.

**How the prototype maps to AK, in one line:** the prototype is a *greenfield* exploration whose **data models and surfaces are workstream-accurate** (Configuration ≈ wizard/profile/capabilities/connectors; Observability ≈ activity feed; Testing ≈ sandbox; Custom Actions ≈ gambit/condition rules), but whose **shell (dashboard-of-agent-cards + bespoke visual builder) is the part the May-18 pivot set aside** in favor of Claude-native patterns. Everything in `project_agent_builder_design_direction_may2026.md` under "What Transfers" is AK-bound; everything under "What Changes" is the shell that AK will re-skin to look like Claude.

## 2. Venli.ai and Sana.ai — what specifically transfers from each

Two outside products were named as references in the May-18 conversation. They are referenced for *different* reasons, and conflating them loses the point.

**Venli.ai** (a small Norwegian hotel-tech startup) is SJ's reference for **the connected-systems natural-language-query use case the team blew past.** His framing: "the use case he nails is we skip that use case — if you connect your systems (loyalty, PMS, CRS, Canary), what questions can you ask it? What dashboards can you build?" The self-criticism attached to it is the load-bearing part: "we got into so much the *agent-builder tab* of Claude… and we skipped the *chat part* of Claude." In other words, the team over-invested in the bespoke builder-with-workflow-cards and under-invested in the much simpler, higher-leverage "connect everything, then just ask it questions and have it build you a view" pattern — which is closer to Claude's actual chat surface. Venli is also framed as an **M&A/investment target** ("possible investment, possible buy-in"), and it anchors SJ's broader directive to **hyper-focus on what the two-person startups in hospitality are doing** — "hyper hyper focus," in his words — and stop fixating on the incumbents (he named **Opera** and **Mews**) and even the smaller players (**Kips**, **Akita** — "maybe not Akita"): *"they don't have any of the baggage that we have."* He suggested a PM spend a few hours a week scouting small hotel-tech startups. [Corrected 2026-06-09: an earlier draft of this sentence rendered SJ's list as "Opera, Mews, Cloudbeds" — "Cloudbeds" was never said; the raw transcript (S2.txt:698) names Opera, Mews, Kips, Akita.] What transfers from Venli: the **chat-as-query-over-connected-systems** paradigm — the thing the prototype's connector architecture *enables* but the prototype's UI never foregrounded.

**Sana.ai** is **Kevin's** reference, and it's about *builder form factor*, not query. Sana's builder is **sequential, with a preview/testing pane side-by-side** — you build a step at a time and watch the result render next to you. Kevin's line: "this sequential is kinda what we have thoughts for agent builder," to which Miguel replied "it's like we never left! 😂 / LOL back to the 3-panel convo." The point: Sana validates the **build-on-the-left, preview-on-the-right** structure that this prototype already used (chat/wizard on the left, the workflow visualizer as the live artifact on the right). It also reinforces the **dual-mode** conclusion — Sana's sequential guided form for beginners, with freeform power for pros, which is exactly the guided-vs-Advanced-Builder split the team had already landed on. What transfers from Sana: the **sequential builder + side-by-side preview** layout, which becomes the skeleton of the Claude-first direction (chat left, artifact/preview pane right).

**Net:** Venli says *what to point the product at* (connected-systems NL query, the chat surface, small-startup focus); Sana says *what shape the builder should take* (sequential, preview-beside-build, dual-mode). Both point the same direction the May-18 pivot already chose — away from a dashboard-of-cards-and-flowcharts and toward a Claude-style chat-plus-artifact experience.

## 3. The chat-first adoption-confidence debate (Kevin ↔ Miguel, May 18)

This is the most important *unresolved-but-aligned* design question for whatever comes after the park. It played out as a Slack exchange the morning of the Q2 Block 2 review.

**The disagreement, stated fairly.** Kevin is **60–70% confident** that chat-as-interface will land with hotel users: "a lot of folks are coming around; ChatGPT and Claude are making chat the interface to interact and build." Miguel's concern is not that he disagrees with the paradigm — he and Kevin are both fluent in it — but that **fluency is exactly the bias**: "while you and I are well-versed in the chat-as-interface paradigm, I wonder what % of our users will know what to do when given this product." He brought **field evidence**: when he demoed the earlier chat-as-builder to a group of AEs and Sales, "a concern that kept being voiced was that hotel users might shun/shy away from this interface and need more hand-holdy onboardings." He flagged it as anecdotal, but it's the strongest signal in the thread because it's from people who sell to hotels.

**The resolution they converged on** (this is the part to carry forward — it is genuinely agreed, not a stalemate):
- **Guided chat, not blank chat.** Kevin: "the chat should feel more hand-holdy over time — just talk to me, I'll figure it out with you." The wow moment has to arrive fast: "while change is hard, we gotta get them to the wow moment quickly." Miguel's gloss: "drag 'em kicking and screaming into the new world."
- **Pre-filled quick replies + clarifying questions, Claude-onboarding style.** Miguel: "giving pre-filled quick replies would be helpful to guide the convo." Kevin: "I like the guided flow from Claude's design where it comes back and asks clarifying [questions]." Miguel: "100% that's where my mind went — one of the easiest onboarding processes." Examples drive completion: "hey, let me walk you through building a quick agent."
- **Both modes, ultimately.** The guided flow is for beginners; a freeform Advanced Builder is for pros. Kevin: "yeah I think we'll need both ultimately." Miguel: "works for me." This re-confirms the guided-vs-Advanced split the prototype already built.
- **Avoid the node-graph trap.** Miguel: "wanna avoid the complexity of agent-builders like ElevenLabs 😂 — we can understand it, but the hotel user will be like 😫." This is the same anti-flowchart instinct that drove the linear-workflow and "chat builds it, you verify it" decisions all the way back in March.
- **A failure they learned from.** Kevin on the RFP agents: "we led with a single massive prompt — didn't make sense and didn't show the nuance." Miguel: "people don't like to read 🤪." The takeaway against both walls-of-text *and* a single mega-prompt: structure the conversation, don't dump.

**Why it matters for the pickup.** The Claude-first direction the team chose is itself a chat-first paradigm — so Miguel's "will hotel users get chat?" concern doesn't disappear with the pivot; it becomes *the* central UX risk. The agreed answer (guided chat + clarifying questions + quick replies + a get-to-wow-fast onboarding + an Advanced escape hatch) is the design brief for the next exploration. The open empirical question Miguel raised — *what percentage of hotel users actually self-serve a chat builder* — is still unanswered and is the thing worth instrumenting first when AK has capacity.

## 4. Priority ranking of the four untasked SDR ideas

Four ideas from the SDR/sales usability test (see `project_agent_builder_sdr_feedback.md`) were captured but never turned into tasks. Ranked here by a design-strategy read of value × validated-demand × build-feasibility × strategic-fit. This is an opinionated recommendation to be re-validated when the work is live, not a locked plan.

**#1 — Call routing by Salesforce account owner.** *If a caller is a known contact assigned to a salesperson in Salesforce, route the call straight to that person and skip the agent.* This ranks first because it is not really a feature — it is the **gate that makes the voice agent acceptable to the highest-value callers at all.** The SDR feedback's cultural-nuance analysis is blunt about it: in entitlement markets and at high-end properties, repeat VIPs and known big-event contacts "won't tolerate AI gatekeeping high-value calls" ("Bobby and I go way back, give me a discount"). Without this gate, the agent doesn't just miss an upsell — it actively *damages* the most valuable relationships by making them argue with a bot. "Personalization isn't just a feature — it's a gate: if the caller is a known high-value contact (via phone number / Salesforce match), skip the agent entirely." It's broadly applicable (any property with a CRM), moderate to build (phone-number ↔ Salesforce-owner match + a routing rule), and it de-risks the core product in exactly the segments where AI adoption is hardest. Ship it as a guardrail on the voice agent, not as a standalone agent.

**#2 — Airline-crew bookings.** *Airlines send Excel sheets of room requirements with no guest info; someone's full-time job is uploading them to the PMS.* This has the **strongest validated demand** of the four — a named customer (a Maui director of sales over four luxury hotels) who said he'd "adopt instantly," automating a literal full-time job, and described as "a standard practice across airline-hotel partnerships," so it generalizes beyond one property. It is a clean, high-ROI back-office automation that fits the agent-builder paradigm directly (a scheduled/ingest trigger → parse spreadsheet → write to PMS, with human-in-the-loop review). It ranks just below Salesforce routing only because it is a **narrower vertical** (group/crew operations) where #1 is a cross-cutting de-risk for the whole voice product. If the team weights named-customer demand above strategic breadth — which is consistent with SJ's "lead with use cases and customers" — this swaps to #1. Either way it's a top-two build.

**#3 — Urgent-call flagging from WORDS, not tone.** *Detect urgency from the specific words a caller uses, never from tonality.* This ranks third on a strong **cost-to-value** basis: messaging already does word-based anger/urgency detection, so voice can **reuse the existing approach** rather than build new ML, and it ships a hard ethical guardrail by construction. The reason tone is off the table is non-negotiable and worth preserving verbatim: **tone-based sentiment analysis is a racial-bias slippery slope** — the SDR's own example was "Hong Kongers sound angry by default," and "AI must not profile people." Words-only urgency flagging improves triage at low risk and low cost; its ceiling is lower than #1/#2 (it's a routing/prioritization aid, not a revenue driver or a full-job automation), which is why it sits third rather than higher. Build it as a small, reusable classifier shared with messaging — and make the words-not-tone rule an explicit, documented constraint so it can't quietly regress into tone detection later.

**#4 — Social-media research for personalization.** *Pull from Instagram / Facebook / LinkedIn to tailor follow-up emails and close deals.* Defer this one. It has the **highest privacy/creepiness risk, the least-clear ROI** ("better tailor the follow-up email"), and it brushes against the **same profiling concern that got tone-detection rejected** — scraping a guest's socials to personalize is a reputational and possibly legal landmine that needs real guardrails before it's worth touching. It is also the hardest of the four to execute well (social data is noisy, identity-matching is error-prone). The value it chases — personalization that closes high-value deals — is better served first by #1 (know your VIPs via the CRM you already have) and #2 (serve the partnerships you already have). Revisit only if a specific, consented, high-value use case emerges with a customer asking for it by name.

**Summary ranking:** 1) Salesforce account-owner routing (gate, not feature — de-risks adoption in the hardest segments) · 2) Airline-crew bookings (strongest validated demand + clear full-job ROI; #1↔#2 is close) · 3) Words-only urgency flagging (cheap reuse + ethical guardrail baked in) · 4) Social-media personalization (defer — privacy/profiling risk, unclear ROI).
